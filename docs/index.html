Swiss Participation in the EU R&I Programmes


The innitiative Stick to Science calls for the rapid re-association of Switzerland and the United Kingdom (UK) to Horizon Europe, the European Framework Programme for research and innovation. For Switzerland, this follows the discontinuation of the country’s participation communicated by the EU in June 17 2021, a month after the launch of Horizon Europe in May 12 2021, and following the cessation of talks on the signing of the Swiss-EU institutional agreement in May 25 2021. Since 2004, thanks to a bilateral agreement with the EU on research and education, Switzerland’s research community has been strongly engaged in the European Research Programmes up until Horizon 2020, which concluded in December 2020. However, statistics on participation are either hard to reproduce—Swiss official figures date back to January 2021 and their data sources are not always openly available—or limited—the EU has not yet published a final evaluation of Horizon 2020, its previous programme, and EU’s new statistics portal, Horizon Dashboard, contains only limited statistics regarding country participation. As an alternative, we aim to analyse Switzerland’s participation in the previous Framework Programmes for research and innovation exclusively based on public project funding and participation data provided by the EU.


Background

Almost one-tenth of the EU Multiannual Financial Framework (MFF) is dedicated to research and innovation. This part of the budget, also known as the Framework Programme (FP) for research and innovation, aims at strengthening the European Research Area.

The Programmes are implemented by the Directorate-General for Research and Innovation and by a few Executive Agencies. FP7 stands for the 7th Framework Programme and corresponds to the MFF 2007-2013. FP8, known as Horizon 2020 (H2020), refers to the 8th Framework Programme and corresponds to the MFF 2014-2020. The ongoing FP, FP9, is also known as Horizon Europe (HE) and corresponds to the MFF 2021-2027.

Switzerland became fully associated to FP6 in 2004. Since 2014, coinciding with the beginning of Horizon 2020, its association status has been challenged. First, as a result of the adoption of the “mass immigration” initiative on February 9 2014, the country was excluded from H2020. In 2015, Switzerland secured partial association and, upon revision of the immigration law in 2017, regained full association. In 2021, the association was once again compromised due to a setback of the negotiations on the Swiss-EU institutional framework agreement.

Swiss Participation Over Time

A closer look at the EU contributions to Switzerland throughout FP7 and H2020 reveals a temporary, yet strong, decline between 2014 and 2017, the time period of exclusion and partial association to H2020.

The record levels of Swiss participation seen in 2022 are due to the time lag between the latest call deadlines in H2020 and starting dates of the projects.

H2020 structure

The Framework Programmes are divided in pillars (or sections) and subdivided in funding instruments (or thematic priorities). H2020, for instance, was composed of three main pillars:

  • Pillar I, on Excellent Science, aiming at enhancing European scientific competitiveness globally, obtained 36% of the budget;

  • Pillar II, on Industrial Leadership, aiming at maximizing competitiveness of European companies and SMEs, obtained 20% of the budget;

  • Pillar III, on Societal Challenges, aiming at tackling Europe’s main political and social priorities, obtained 39% of the budged.

Compared to the overall budget distribution for all countries, the distribution EU contributions obtained by Switzerland in H2020 is strongly skewed towards the pillar on Excellent Science (64.3% for Switzerland versus 36.2% for all countries).

In addition, contributions from the European Research Council (ERC), the flagship instrument of pillar on Excellent Science, in which scientific excellence is the sole selection criterion, accounted for more than 40% of all EU contributions to Switzerland. In relative terms, the share of ERC contributions to Switzerland surpasses the share of contributions from the entire pillar on Excellent Science to all countries.

Participation profile

Among the top 15 participating countries in H2020, Switzerland, Israel and the United Kingdom were the only countries which obtained more than half of EU contributions from the pillar on Excellence Science. This underlines both the strength and importance of scientific research in these three countries, as well as the engagement of their research communities in the Framework Programme.

As expected, higher shares of contributions from the pillar on Excellent Science typically reflect higher shares of contributions to institutions in research and higher education, as opposed to contributions to private for profit companies.

Switzerland and the UK were the only countries in which institutions in research and higher education also obtained over three-quarters of EU contributions. This, again, underlines both the strength and importance of the institutions in research and higher education in the two countries and their involvement in the Programme.

Outlook

Compared to Horizon 2020, Horizon Europe’s budget shows that the EU appears to have divested from the pillar on Excellent Science in both relative and absolute terms. Furthermore, Switzerland and the UK have only had limited access to Horizon Europe as non-associated countries to date, given the stopped talks on June 2021 for Switzerland and the unclear path post-Brexit for the UK. If they remain largely excluded, Horizon Europe may prove a step back in terms of global competitiveness for European research. The two countries have been top participants in the pillar on Excellent Science and therefore important contributors to the competitiveness of European research.

For researchers affiliated to Swiss institutions, the exclusion from Horizon Europe results in either unfavorable conditions of participation (eg. no project coordination), or unequivocal exclusion from funding instruments. Exclusion particular concerns most of the pillar on Excellent Science, which has also been the most important pillar for Switzerland in the preceding Programmes.

The exclusion of Switzerland from Horizon Europe is blocking a win-win agreement on research collaboration. In combination with the exclusion of the UK, it may result in a major setback to the path for strengthening the European Research Area. Therefore the Stick to Science initiative concerns as much the interests of Switzerland and the UK as the defense of European research as a whole. Therefore it deserves our unconditional support.
